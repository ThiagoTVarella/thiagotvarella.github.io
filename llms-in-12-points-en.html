Large Language Models, in 12 Points

English translation by Thiago Tarraf Varella of an article originally written in Portuguese with Alan Barzilay and Ramon Vilarino, published by Nexo Políticas Públicas (June 2024). This translation aims to be faithful to the original; footnote numbering matches the Portuguese version.

Large language models, or LLMs, entered the popular vocabulary at the end of 2022, causing a great deal of impact and discussion. This is no accident, since these models do things that seemed unbelievable just a few years ago. One piece of evidence for their capability is that some of them, like OpenAI's GPT-4, can answer questions that take humans years of medical or law school to be able to answer. Many people saw enormous potential in this: in just five days after launch, OpenAI's service for trying out its language model, ChatGPT, registered a million users. A year after launch, it is estimated that the site is visited by hundreds of millions of people every week.[1]

But what are these LLMs? In short, they are computer programs that take in a piece of text and produce another, as if they were having a conversation: the program reads a question and produces an answer. Through complex algorithms, the model manages to predict the most likely next word based on the sentence that came before it — for example, it might respond to "see you later!" with another "see you!". Still, some questions deserve attention. Do these models talk the way we do, or do they deserve our trust?

In the questions that follow, we cover the economic and social impacts, the infrastructure behind these models, and other topics from this complex and recent world of large language models.

1. How did language become a problem in computer science?

In science, models are generally simplified representations of something we want to study, much like a map is a representation of a given place. One of the earliest attempts known to Western science to model language comes from Aristotle, who introduced a system for determining whether sentences were logically correct or not.[2] This Aristotelian logical system was later expanded into the theory of formal systems, in which a language is defined by its vocabulary and by a set of rules defining how the elements of that vocabulary can interact with one another — its grammar.[3]

More recently, starting in the 20th century, scientists introduced models that tried to characterize these rules by looking at how frequently elements of the vocabulary appear together[4], known as statistical language models. For example, if one of these models predicts one letter at a time and is trained on the word "batata" (Portuguese for "potato"), it would learn that the letter "t" is always followed by "a."

Useful as they were, over time scientists found flaws in these models, such as sentences that are grammatical but make no sense.[5] Even so, models inspired by the frequency of word sequences opened the door to a field of computer science called Natural Language Processing (NLP). These techniques came to be used, for example, to classify emails as spam or to build phone autocorrect features. In particular, the introduction of neural networks into this field further expanded the potential of language models, and at the foundation of these models lies the numerical representation of words.

2. How do words become numbers?

Language models are computer programs, and as such they perform operations on bits (numbers represented as electronic signals). So the first step to understanding how they work is understanding how to represent language — not just words — as bits. Attentive readers might ask: "in a computer, everything is represented by numbers like 0s and 1s. Can't we just work with that?" As good as this way of representing text is for computers, it falls short for LLMs.

We could use that strategy to write any possible sequence of characters, whether it forms a word or is just a random sequence. That tremendous flexibility isn't really necessary, since we don't need to handle every possible sequence of letters, just the ones that form actual words. We can build a more optimized representation for dealing with real languages. For example, we'd like similar words, or words with similar meanings, to be represented in some similarly related way. The best solution found so far for this problem is known as embedding. This technique represents each word as a list of numbers, ideally in a way that "captures" interesting properties of its relationship to other words.

One option would be to choose similar numbers for similar words. A seminal algorithm in the field, word2vec[6][7], does this by processing the context around the word of interest, so that words used in similar contexts end up with similar representations. This process rests on the idea that we can infer a word's meaning from its context. This type of embedding, trained by the word2vec algorithm, turned out to be capable of representing synonyms in similar ways. It was even possible to represent analogies through mathematical operations on word representations. Using embeddings, we can turn words into numbers efficiently enough for LLMs to process text. That's why the better our representation of language is, the more powerful and flexible the LLM that uses it will be.

3. How is an LLM (Large Language Model) built?

Public-facing chat LLMs, like ChatGPT, go through three stages of training: neural network training, a supervised fine-tuning stage, and a reinforcement-learning refinement stage (also called RLHF).[8][9] Neural networks are models that take numbers as input, perform a series of mathematical operations, and return other numbers as output. One difference here is that the numbers represent words (see question 2). Just like the simple statistical models from question 1, the words a model returns are the ones most likely to appear as a continuation of the input text.

Neural networks can have very different structures, commonly referred to as network architecture, depending on the task they'll be used for. Currently, the most prominent LLMs use an architecture called "transformers."[10] Training transformers is the most computationally expensive step. In this process, the team training the model feeds it an enormous amount of text — an amount that would take a human thousands of years to read without stopping to eat or sleep. For each stretch of this text, the model tries to predict the next word, and it's corrected whenever the prediction is wrong. A distinguishing feature of transformer models, one that improved on earlier neural network designs, is that they assign numerical importance to specific parts of the input text. For example, consider the sentence "yesterday she graduated from medical school, today she's already working as a…" For the model to predict the word "doctor," it needs to weigh the words "she" and "medical school" heavily, and doesn't need to weigh "graduated" or "today" nearly as much. This technique — predicting not just the next word, but also which earlier words matter most — is called attention, and it's one of the most defining features of transformer networks.

The next stage of training, supervised fine-tuning, is when the model is trained to follow instructions and answer questions. In a sense, the model is still just continuing the input text, but now it does so in a way that creates the impression that the answer is a distinct piece of text from the question, rather than just any continuation. Training works similarly to the previous stage, except the dataset here is built by human workers who write example questions along with ideal versions of what an answer should look like. For instance, in the earlier stage, if the input text was "yesterday I went to Paris and visited," the model would continue with a list of Paris tourist attractions, as if it were the person who had typed the sentence. After fine-tuning, the model would instead respond with something like "if you have questions about Paris, I can help," since it's now trained to generate answers rather than mere continuations. This stage is what lets humans interact with the model as a conversational (chat) model, with questions and answers, instead of a generic language model.

The final training stage, reinforcement learning from human feedback, is what enabled these models' explosive growth at the end of 2022.[8] In this stage, the model is trained to respond in ways that please the person asking the question. During this refinement, the model generates several candidate answers to a question, and the team has human raters judge which candidate is best. In ChatGPT's case, raters had to rank answers across several categories — for example: does the generated text actually answer the question? Does it contain false information? Does it promote sexual or violent content? These evaluations are then used to adjust the model so it becomes "aligned" with what a human would say — or rather, with what the team believes a human would want to hear.

4. Do LLMs learn the way I learn?

The training process of an LLM is also called "learning." Still, it's obviously different from how a human learns — computers don't have real neurons, for instance. Even so, there are genuine similarities, and that's no accident: machine learning researchers frequently look to biology and psychology for inspiration. For example, the term "statistical learning" is used in both psychology and computer science. In LLMs, it describes the fact that models are trained to identify and predict patterns in language through repeated exposure to text. In psychology, the term also describes pattern identification[11], though with important differences. For instance, a baby is understood to learn to associate the word "woof" after hearing it while looking at a dog, hearing it bark, or feeling its fur. Researchers describe these experiences as becoming integrated to form the concept of "dog." Once the associations are well established, hearing the word "woof" or an actual bark are equally valid ways to bring the concept to mind. So while in humans the associations form between words and concepts, in LLMs the associations form between syllables and other syllables.

Another important difference is that human language is learned through a social process.[12] A child learns what a "woof" is before learning the word "dog," and much earlier than learning the word "pack." This shapes how each person relates to these concepts. Some people associate dogs with positive experiences, others with negative ones, and these feelings are also part of the concept a person develops of "dog." Language models don't learn to associate words with experiences. The order in which they're exposed to words isn't especially relevant, since the same texts get presented to the model repeatedly. Even so, one could argue that a different learning process doesn't matter, as long as the observed outcome is the same. What remains to be determined, then, are the differences in how language is actually produced between a model and a human being.

5. Do LLMs talk the way I talk?

Although LLMs can be very good at fooling humans, an LLM's language production is still distinct from a human's. As we saw in question 4, learning language is an experience that isn't just symbolic (represented by symbols, like letters and words), but also semantic (words have meanings) and sensory (words relate to our senses).

Each of these factors shapes how humans choose their next words. For example, in Portuguese, after saying several words ending in "-ada," like "estrada" (road) or "pousada" (inn), people often answer the question "what do we put in the toaster" with "torrada" (toast) rather than "pão" (bread). This reveals a sensory association: the sounds we've just been hearing make "torrada" easier to recall. This is an example of what's called a production error. Another example would be answering "milk" after hearing the word "white" many times and being asked "what does a cow drink" — even while knowing the real answer is water. This happens because of semantic associations we build over our lives between the words "white," "cow," and "milk."[13]

Language models don't have the ability to form such associations, and so they don't make these particular kinds of errors. Still, they produce their own distinct errors.[14] For example, language models frequently fail at counting tasks, like counting how many times the letter "a" appears in a given paragraph.[15] That makes sense, since this isn't a task you can do just by predicting the next word — even though it's something humans do easily. So despite the similarities, like correct grammar, these distinct kinds of errors show that language production has a radically different nature in humans than it does in LLMs.

6. Is producing text the same as understanding language?

Talking with today's models can feel like talking to a person, but does it make sense to think these models truly understand what's being said? One way to explore this question is the Turing test, proposed as a way to check whether a program had achieved "intelligence."[16] It works by having a human take part in two conversations by message — one with a second human, one with a program — without knowing beforehand which is which. If the human can't tell the program apart, that would imply the machine had achieved an intelligence indistinguishable from a human's, since it would be capable of "understanding" our language and communicating naturally. A common criticism of this test is that it's only interested in fooling a human, which is different from testing comprehension.

When we communicate, we rarely use sentences that have never been said before. For example, when we run into someone, we use some variation of "hi, how are you?" and respond with equally routine platitudes. Sometimes the response "I'm good" doesn't even carry meaning beyond signaling that communication has been established. This illustrates the potential for communication to work using nothing but established patterns, regardless of meaning. So would reproducing patterns of language count as evidence of understanding? This question sits at the heart of some pointed criticisms of today's language models. Researchers argue it's impossible for these models to learn meaning while being trained only to capture the statistical patterns of a language.[17]

The way LLMs are built is enough to create machines that produce text that seems to make sense, but current models include no process that ties meaning to language: form isn't enough to learn meaning.

What's more, the meaning of a text doesn't exist in a vacuum. The reader isn't a passive party in communication, but actively seeks to interpret and assign coherence to whatever their interlocutor says, whether or not it makes sense on its own. Human communication is always mediated by the impressions and expectations between the people communicating. For example, when we read a text, we always draw on what we know about the writer or the medium to give the message context. The sentences generated by a model, therefore, carry no intrinsic meaning. It's the humans interacting with the model who assign that meaning. The fact that LLMs produce text doesn't mean they understand language.

7. Can you trust an LLM?

We can't trust LLMs for a range of tasks, like telling the truth or avoiding statements that perpetuate injustice. LLMs invent information and sources while giving off an illusion of objectivity. There's no shortage of examples of "AI" models[18][19][20][21] behaving problematically. As text generators, LLMs are just as capable of producing useful, illuminating sentences as they are of producing false, confusing, meaningless, offensive, and unjust content — commonly referred to as hallucinations.

However great the efforts to eliminate hallucinations, nothing can guarantee they won't happen. There's reason to believe they will never fully stop unless we fundamentally change how these models are built.[22] We don't even have reliable methods to detect them: the only way to check whether an answer is correct is to have humans manually validate answers one by one.

If a doctor told you that you needed surgery, you'd probably want to know why, since we expect answers and explanations for decisions that get made.[23] Humans are able to explain how they arrived at an answer — something LLMs can emulate, but are fundamentally incapable of actually doing. This problem is common to machine-learning-based models, whose inner workings are opaque to interpretation. If it's not possible to know why a model produced a specific answer, its behavior is essentially unpredictable, and trusting it in important contexts can have disastrous consequences.

It's no coincidence that there are many cases of machine-learning-based systems automatically intensifying and scaling up injustice and inequality. In healthcare, risk classifiers built on this paradigm prioritized treatment for white patients over sicker Black patients.[24] In the justice system, algorithms used by judges to assess "risk of reoffending" have been shown to be biased toward convicting Black people and acquitting white people[25], and in the Brazilian banking system, there's at least one notable example of a credit-scoring system that favored consumers in proportion to how many white people lived in their region.[26] In the same way, whether hallucinating or not, LLMs carry the potential to spread and perpetuate unjust representations and hate speech, which, combined with their aura of objectivity, contributes to the ongoing subjugation of historically marginalized groups.

Despite the enthusiasm around these technologies, caution is needed. Answers generated by LLMs aren't the product of reasoning or logical thought — they're simply the reproduction of statistical patterns, which isn't enough to operate language reliably. Imagine a school that decides to generate its textbooks or exam questions with an LLM and no human review. It wouldn't take long before new textbooks were claiming that water can transmit Parkinson's disease.[27] All of this means that using LLMs for consequential decisions without human oversight is irresponsible, and potentially even criminal.

8. What are the social and environmental implications of an LLM's infrastructure?

Developing large language models depends on a multinational, multibillion-dollar infrastructure. To give a sense of scale, a single version of ChatGPT reportedly cost more than R$500 million (Brazilian reais, roughly US$100 million at the time) to train[28] — not counting the cost of running and maintaining the model, or the network of supercomputers, which itself costs on the order of billions.[29] There's also an environmental cost that can't be ignored. It's estimated that training a single LLM consumes enough energy to power thousands of homes for an entire year.[30][31] On top of that, cooling the computers uses an enormous amount of water, on the order of millions of liters per month.[33] As a result, the use of LLMs has raised concerns about strain on the electrical grid[32] and water supply[37] in the US. The impact goes beyond financial and environmental cost. The huge quantity of data used to build these models requires extensive curation, which companies handle by employing workers under precarious conditions, for extremely low pay, in poor regions of Asia and Africa.[40] All of these issues are, to some extent, tied to the technology behind LLMs.

Building and running LLMs relies on recognizing and exploiting patterns present in text (see questions 1 through 5). This paradigm only became capable of producing genuinely useful text once models grew large enough to require massive amounts of data.[39] In April 2024, Meta announced an LLM trained on around 10 trillion words, and the model behind ChatGPT performs trillions of mathematical operations to generate a single word.[41] It's this scale of data processing that requires extremely expensive supercomputers, whose operation demands so many water and electrical resources. To gather this much data, the corporations building these models scour large portions of the internet, which invariably contains hate speech and inappropriate, violent, or offensive content. Limiting the chances of LLMs producing that kind of content requires manual curation work at an industrial scale. The workers responsible for this are exposed to potentially traumatic material and are still poorly paid by companies that try every which way to cut costs.[35] At the edge of this iceberg, there's also the near-total disregard for legal issues like copyright and privacy.[38][34]

Only organizations able to mobilize this scale of resources and financial power are capable of producing an LLM. It's no accident that this race has been dominated by Big Tech, reinforcing the oligopolistic character the sector currently has. This concentration has already worried a number of governments, which have taken action to curb the monopoly power of large tech conglomerates like Microsoft, Google, and Meta.[36] We can't deny that the introduction of LLMs carries several implications, like possible restructurings of language (see question 10) or of the economy (see question 11). If so few players are capable of building these models, the power to shape these restructurings sits in the hands of very few people. A mode of technology production implying this level of exclusivity reveals the political character of these technological advances.

9. Who owns an LLM?

In April 2024, Meta — the company behind Facebook, WhatsApp, and Instagram — launched a new LLM, Llama 3.[42] Although it didn't demonstrate any new or surprising ability, Llama 3 generated excitement among enthusiasts and developers because of how the company makes its models available. Meta releases its LLMs for free commercial use (except to other tech giants), making them open so that anyone can install and modify them in any way.[43][44] By contrast, OpenAI, the company behind ChatGPT, keeps its models confidential, and its revenue comes precisely from charging for access to them. Open, free models don't just lower the cost of developing new LLM-based applications — they also let other teams try to build better models using them as a starting point.[45]

The decision about whether to release a model is a key point in shaping its place in society. The infrastructure needed to build an LLM requires investing a huge amount of resources (see questions 3 and 8). And yet, without the enormous amount of text freely available on the internet, produced by all of us, those investments would be irrelevant. LLMs are defined by the exploitation of an essentially collective (and generally public) resource by institutions capable of mobilizing large amounts of resources. There's therefore an argument that LLMs should be treated as a collective good, since their raw material comes from all of our (involuntary) contributions, and the text they generate is essentially a form of reproducing what's already been written. This tension recently played out when Stack Overflow, a forum where programmers help each other with computing problems, announced a deal making its users' posts available for OpenAI to improve its LLMs and products, which are neither open nor free.[46] Some users, upset at the appropriation of their contributions, reacted by deleting their posts and were retaliated against with bans from the forum[47] — a similar deal was struck by Reddit, another major social network.[48]

As much as free, open LLMs are a step in the right direction, the power over the decisions that steer the technical progress of these models remains restricted. It rests in the hands of the very few people who control the small number of organizations capable of collecting and processing the enormous quantities of data these models require. Despite appearing liberal, Meta's stance is still a business strategy that positions it at the epicenter of influence over the development and use of LLMs, while weakening the business model of its main competitors. The dominance of large corporations in producing LLMs marks the growing loss of influence held by universities and research institutes over technological progress. As a result, research and innovation in this area are increasingly steered by financial-market interests. This is an especially hostile environment for the emergence and development of technologies that don't follow today's logic of centralization and exploitation. Yet these tools have consequences for our lives that range from the dynamics of work to our relationship with language and artistic production. It would therefore be in everyone's interest for their development to happen as democratically as possible.

10. What language does an LLM speak?

LLMs bring into focus the specific features of language that they're able to mimic. However objective a piece of computer software might seem, the forms of language captured and represented by language models result from decisions about their design and construction — and are therefore the result of an essentially political process. A key example of the decisions involved in developing an LLM lies in the selection, curation, documentation (or lack thereof), and disclosure (or lack thereof) of its training data. These choices carry crucial social consequences, ranging from the privilege given to certain languages to the spread of hate speech and intolerant worldviews (see question 7). Given the supposed omnipresence of LLMs in our daily lives, promoted by this technology's makers and enthusiasts, these decisions carry the concerning potential to substantially shape how we communicate and express ourselves.

In the process of Brazil's formation, there's an important milestone that helps illustrate how political the processes determining the language we speak really are — and how the language we speak shapes political processes in turn. In the 1750s, Portugal enacted the Directorate Law, which required, among other things, the use of the Portuguese language throughout its territory as a way of consolidating the Crown's rule in the Americas.[49] Over the first 200 years of colonization, Jesuit missions held considerable influence over Indigenous communities, having established communication with native peoples in their own languages. As a result, the missions had come to function almost like a parallel power, and the Crown was losing influence and control over economic processes, missing out on valuable resources. Making Portuguese mandatory was a key piece in the metropole's reassertion of control, and it decisively shaped the formation of Brazil and its identity. By erasing many other languages spoken across its territory from institutional life, this process set Brazil apart from other nation-building projects — Paraguay, for example, where Guarani is an official language spoken by most of the population.[50][51] Still, the process of standardizing language is neither finished nor settled; it remains in constant dispute and construction. A good example is the role television news programs and soap operas have played, since the arrival of television, in establishing a reference for what a "neutral" Brazilian dialect would sound like.

Today, LLMs bring a new possibility for shaping language in institutional life — not by decree, but through the possibility of fundamentally restructuring how we communicate. As we come to understand the place these tools will occupy in our lives and in society, their makers promote them as revolutionary. We're promised that integrating LLMs into text editors will produce precise, ideal writing from minimal instructions, and that LLM-powered messaging services will make us more sociable by adjusting a message's tone to be friendlier, more professional, or simply more fun. As we're seduced and pressured into these adoptions in the name of greater efficiency, we gradually hand over the power to standardize language to these tools. If we trust LLMs to tell us the ideal shape of a text, or to define what counts as a friendly tone versus a professional one, we hand them the potential to reorient and redefine what counts as acceptable language in everyday life, institutional or not. Evidence of this process can already be seen in a subtle but notable shift in the words used in scientific papers since ChatGPT's launch.[52]

The technical conditions behind how LLMs are built carry important limitations on which aspects of language they'll reproduce and represent. The leading LLMs currently available work more effectively in English, due to the prevalence of that language on the internet — itself a reflection of how control over technology production and access is concentrated in a handful of countries. As an inherent consequence of their current technology, LLMs remain highly prone to producing text that's false, offensive, or unjust (see question 7). The measures meant to reduce the odds of that happening — beyond relying on the exploitation of thousands of underpaid workers (see question 8) — also involve subtle decisions about how to appropriately represent notions of fairness and ethical principles. But the notions and principles represented by these models are those of the very small number of people who control the handful of organizations actually capable of collecting and processing the massive amount of data an LLM requires. Broadly speaking, it falls to these same few people to decide the forms of expression and language that will come to dominate our interactions with these models.

11. What are the economic impacts of LLMs?

The US stock market saw a strong rebound in investment in 2023, following a 2022 downturn. American stocks posted impressive growth between October 2022 — their low point that year — and the end of 2023. Interestingly, more than 70% of that growth was concentrated in the stock of a very small number of companies, distinguished by their heavy involvement with LLMs and the infrastructure technologies behind them.[53] The start of that rebound coincides with the launch of ChatGPT in November 2022, the most famous LLM-based product. Among these companies, the most emblematic case is Nvidia. The leading manufacturer of processing units essential to training LLMs saw its stock triple in value over the past year, pushing past a $3 trillion market valuation.[54] Such investor enthusiasm might suggest that the technological milestone of LLMs also signals a major economic transformation.

Relevant as they are, stock market movements above all reflect the expectations and speculation of investors. In the recent past, investor euphoria — and a failure to recognize how premature and exaggerated their expectations were — culminated in enormous losses from immature, naive bets on the revolution that broad internet access would supposedly bring.[55] It's important, then, to view this sector's expectations with some skepticism. Goldman Sachs, the world's second-largest investment bank, published a study in 2023 with estimates for the impact of LLMs and similar models on the economy. Among other things, the study projects a 1.5 percentage-point increase in US productive capacity.[56] While it's arguable that growth of this magnitude is a reasonable thing to attach to the introduction of a relevant new technology[57][58], much of this economic enthusiasm comes from a belief in these technologies' ability to fully or partially automate jobs — an ability that hasn't yet been proven.

However capable LLMs are of generating useful, illuminating sentences, they aren't capable of reasoning, interpretation, decision-making, or judgment, and all of those faculties are essential to occupations that require language-based interaction. It's no accident that premature attempts to automate customer service have ended in disastrous results.[59] LLMs capture how words relate to each other statistically, which turns out to be surprisingly enough to produce sentences we find meaningful, and even functional code. That, however, isn't enough to engage in real conversation or any language-based decision process. What's more, even in relatively simple cases like manufacturing, large-scale full automation is complex. It involves deep adaptation of already-established systems and processes, which can be costly and time-consuming for large corporations.

Impressive as LLM output can be, there still aren't clear alternative ways to generate value with them beyond the writing assistants their makers offer and occasionally dysfunctional chatbots. And given how unreliable they are for operating autonomously, at least for now, their impact on production is likely to come through supporting human work rather than any full replacement — something that's difficult to quantify. It's possible investor expectations will pan out this time — there are some important differences from what happened during the dot-com bubble[60] — but for now, any estimate of LLMs' economic impact remains an essentially speculative exercise.

12. How do you regulate an LLM?

Introducing LLMs into our daily lives, far from simply solving problems, brings complex interactions and new institutional problems along with it. For example, in an attempt to help and guide New York City residents on local regulations, the city government made a chatbot available. Among various absurd answers, the chatbot told one user it's legal to sell human meat for consumption in the city.[61][62] In another episode, a car dealership's chatbot negotiated the sale of a new car to a user for one dollar.[63] Also notable was the case of Air Canada, which was ordered to refund a customer who had followed erroneous instructions from its customer-service chatbot.[64] How do you establish responsibility, requirements, and consequences in situations like these? These examples point to the serious challenges that adopting LLMs into service infrastructure poses for the legal system.

Even interactions with laws designed for data technologies aren't yet well worked out. Brazil's General Data Protection Law (LGPD), designed to protect the use, exploitation, and handling of personal data, is one example of the scale of the challenges at hand. Conceptually, LLMs aren't built to handle personal data. But insofar as these models don't refrain from generating text containing personal information, they are subject to LGPD legislation. Even the simplest of their applications, like chats that let you interact directly with the models, currently violate that legislation.[65] The stochastic behavior of LLMs, the massive scale at which they process data, and the wide range of possible applications represent a crucial regulatory challenge — one that's difficult to compare to what other technologies have presented.

A regulatory strategy needs to balance several challenges. It has to be general enough for its application to be broad, and specific enough to clearly determine what counts as compliance. That specificity, though, shouldn't make the regulatory mechanism prone to rapid obsolescence. This makes it tempting — and easier — to discuss the legal challenges of such a broad, fast-moving technology only in general, abstract terms. Recently, a summit bringing together dozens of countries to discuss the risks and regulatory needs of artificial intelligence[67], organized by the British government, was criticized precisely for failing to outline concrete paths toward regulatory mechanisms, as noted by professor Luca Belli of FGV-RJ.[68]

In Brazil, discussions are starting to take shape into viable bills that consolidate progress in defining concepts, foundations, and principles.[69] But these bills fall short precisely where it comes to concretely establishing institutional responsibilities and mechanisms for enforcement, oversight, and technical standard-setting. In Europe, the AI Act goes a bit further, defining a regulatory body responsible for integrating and aligning AI regulation with existing regulatory systems.[70][71] Still, European regulators face difficulty setting clear technical standards that objectively define legal compliance. This difficulty is inherent to the technology itself, since an LLM's exact behavior is essentially unpredictable. There are no robust techniques to guarantee that LLMs won't produce false, confusing, meaningless, offensive, or unjust content, nor established ways to measure the likelihood that they will (see question 7).
