Caramelo

A science-backed daily math practice app for Brazilian high school students preparing for the ENEM. Built on retrieval practice, spaced repetition (FSRS), AI-powered scaffolding, and gamification — designed as a rigorous experimental platform to investigate how optimized AI tutoring drives learning outcomes and narrows the achievement gap.

Core thesis: The most important factor in AI tutoring is not whether students get AI help, but exactly how that help is designed. Caramelo tests this by running randomized controlled trials embedded in a real product used by real students in Brazilian schools.


Dual-city launch: São José do Rio Preto (SP) & Natal (RN) — complementary markets testing willingness-to-pay (SP) and retention at scale (RN).

Four interrelated studies addressing complementary questions at different levels of the learning system. All studies share the same app, question bank, and gamification — only the experimental variable changes.

Study 1: AI Scaffolding Strategies in Intelligent Tutoring Systems

5-arm RCT 3º ano (12th grade) N = 1000 8 weeks

Does the design of AI scaffolding strategy significantly affect mathematics learning outcomes among Brazilian high school students preparing for the ENEM?

Literature Context

Two 2025 RCTs reached opposite conclusions about AI math tutoring. Kestin et al. (Nature Scientific Reports) showed a custom AI tutor at Harvard produced 2x learning gains over active learning classrooms. Bastani et al. (PNAS) found Turkish high schoolers using ChatGPT-4 scored 17% lower on closed-book exams. The key difference: Kestin's tutor was architecturally constrained (brief, one step at a time, expert-verified paths). Bastani's gave unrestricted access (51% accuracy, 42% logical errors). The intervention is the constraint, not the model.

Hypotheses
  • H1 (Structured > Naive): All structured scaffolding conditions (Socratic, step-by-step, ZPD-adaptive) will produce significantly greater learning gains than naive AI. Replicates Bastani et al. in a new context.
  • H2 (ZPD-adaptive > Fixed): ZPD-adaptive scaffolding will outperform fixed strategies, particularly at the extremes of the ability distribution (expertise reversal effect).
  • H3 (Strategy x Ability Interaction): Step-by-step benefits low-ability students more; Socratic benefits high-ability students more (cognitive load theory).
  • H4 (Naive = No Improvement): Naive AI will produce no significant improvement over static explanations.
Experimental Arms
ArmLabelBehavior After Wrong Answer
1StaticPre-authored step-by-step explanation. No interactivity.
2Naive AIMinimal prompt ("You are a helpful math tutor"). No constraints. Replicates Bastani "GPT Base."
3Socratic AIGuiding questions only. Never reveals the answer directly. Has verified solution path internally.
4Step-by-step AIExpert-verified solution revealed one step at a time. Pauses to check understanding. Replicates Kestin (2025).
5ZPD-adaptive AIDynamically selects strategy based on Elo: worked examples for novices, guided hints for intermediates, Socratic prompts for advanced. Fades as mastery increases.
Methodology

5-arm, double-blind, parallel-group, school-stratified RCT. Pre-registered. 180+ ENEM math questions, identical across all arms. 3 questions/day for 8 weeks. Pre/post 20-item assessment (supervised, on paper). Voluntary ENEM score follow-up in November.

Analysis Plan

ITT analysis. One-way ANCOVA (post-test ~ condition + pre-test). Planned contrasts: structured vs. naive, ZPD-adaptive vs. other structured, static vs. naive. 5×3 factorial ANCOVA for ability interaction. Multilevel growth models on daily accuracy. Multiple imputation (m=20) under MAR. Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction.

Power

N=75 per group after attrition. 80% power to detect d=0.32 pairwise, f=0.13 omnibus (alpha=0.05, pre-post r=0.50).

Expected Results

If H2 is supported (ZPD-adaptive > fixed), this would be the first experimental evidence that dynamically adaptive scaffolding outperforms fixed strategies in AI tutoring. If not supported, simpler scaffolding suffices — a practically valuable finding for resource-constrained deployments. If H3 is supported (strategy x ability interaction), the equity implications are strong: adaptive scaffolding's primary value is personalization for students most poorly served by one-size-fits-all instruction.

Study 2: Adaptive Question Selection and the Zone of Proximal Development

3-arm RCT 2º ano (11th grade) N = 225 12 weeks

Does ZPD-targeted question selection, maintaining ~85% success rate via Elo-based difficulty matching, produce greater learning gains than simpler selection methods?

Literature Context

Wilson et al. (2019, Nature Communications) proved that the optimal error rate for learning is approximately 15% (85% success rate). The Elo rating system (functionally equivalent to the Rasch model) can target this rate in real time. FSRS optimizes when to review but not which item to present when multiple are due. No published study has experimentally tested whether targeting 85% via Elo produces superior learning in a real educational application.

Hypotheses
  • H1 (ZPD vs. Random): ZPD-targeted selection will produce greater pre-to-post learning gains than random selection. Random selection exposes students to items too easy or too hard with equal probability.
  • H2 (ZPD vs. FSRS-only): ZPD-targeted selection will outperform pure FSRS. FSRS optimizes timing but not difficulty.
  • H3 (Engagement): ZPD selection will show higher session completion and daily return rates (flow theory).
  • H4 (Heterogeneous effect): ZPD advantage will be larger for students with high variance in topic-level ability ("jagged profiles").
Experimental Arms
ArmLabelSelection Logic
1RandomRandom from available pool with topic diversity constraint. Uniform without replacement.
2FSRS-onlyStandard FSRS scheduling. Lowest retrievability first. No difficulty matching.
3ZPD-adaptiveElo-based difficulty matching + FSRS spacing. Among due items, selects where P(correct) ≈ 0.85. Tolerance: 0.70–0.95.
Methodology

3-arm, single-blind, parallel-group RCT. Stratified by classroom and baseline score terciles. Same question bank, same scaffolding (fixed step-by-step AI), same gamification. 12-week duration (Elo needs ~50 responses to stabilize, then 10 weeks of calibrated practice).

Analysis Plan

One-way ANCOVA with Bonferroni-corrected contrasts (alpha=0.0167). Multilevel growth models on daily accuracy. Mixed-effects model testing ZPD convergence to 85%. Discrete-time survival analysis for churn. Bayesian sensitivity analysis with weakly informative priors.

Power

N=75 per arm, ANCOVA with r=0.60: 80% power for d=0.46 pairwise.

Expected Results

Anticipated graded outcome: ZPD-adaptive > FSRS-only > Random. The ZPD-FSRS comparison is most informative because the two systems optimize conflicting objectives (an overdue easy item has high FSRS priority but low ZPD priority). If ZPD does not outperform, the complexity of Elo matching is unjustified — random selection suffices.

Study 3: Metacognitive and Psychological Interventions in Digital Practice

2×2×2 Factorial 1º ano (10th grade) N = 400 8 weeks

Do metacognitive prompts and psychological interventions improve learning outcomes and engagement when deployed as toggleable features in a daily math practice app?

Literature Context

Metacognition ranks as the single highest-effect influence on learning (d=1.33–1.44, Hattie 2009). The hypercorrection effect shows high-confidence errors are corrected most durably (Butterfield & Metcalfe, 2001). Process praise (Mueller & Dweck, 1998) promotes resilience, though broader "growth mindset" interventions show weak effects (d=0.05). Implementation intentions increase goal completion (d=0.65). These interventions are well-supported in labs but rarely tested in consumer-facing digital products where engagement is voluntary and friction is lethal.

Experimental Factors
FactorONOFF
A: Confidence RatingBefore answering, rate confidence: "Chutando / Mais ou menos / Tenho certeza". Calibration feedback shown after.No confidence prompt.
B: Pre-Quest PredictionBefore daily quest, predict how many of 3 correct. See calibration feedback after.No prediction prompt.
C: Process PraiseAfter correct answers, specific strategy praise (e.g., "You broke that problem down systematically").Neutral "Correto!" feedback.

Implementation intentions collected from ALL students during onboarding as observational variable (not experimentally manipulated).

Hypotheses
  • H1: Confidence ratings improve calibration accuracy over time.
  • H2: Confidence ratings improve retention for high-confidence errors (hypercorrection).
  • H3: Pre-quest prediction improves metacognitive awareness and post-test performance.
  • H4: Process praise increases persistence and willingness to attempt harder questions.
  • H5: Three-way interaction is nonsignificant (effects are additive).
  • H6: Implementation intention specificity predicts 30-day engagement.
Methodology

2×2×2 between-subjects factorial (8 cells, 50 per cell). 1º ano students — furthest from ENEM, allowing pure learning effects without test-anxiety confounds. Same questions, selection algorithm, AI scaffolding, gamification, and interface except toggled features. Mid-point calibration probe at Week 4.

Analysis Plan

2×2×2 ANCOVA with pre-test covariate. Mixed-effects model on weekly calibration scores. Logistic mixed-effects for hypercorrection. Cox proportional hazards for churn. Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction. Pre-registered on AsPredicted or OSF.

Power

N=50 per cell: 80% power for main effects f=0.175, two-way interactions f=0.20, three-way interaction f=0.25.

Expected Results

Confidence ratings expected to show the strongest learning effect via hypercorrection. The key finding may concern engagement: if confidence ratings reduce session completion, this quantifies the metacognitive-friction tradeoff for the first time in a real product. The three-way interaction may reveal "metacognitive overload" if all features ON shows lower engagement than the additive prediction.

Study 4: Stakeholder Perceptions of AI Tutoring — A Qualitative Investigation

Qualitative All grades N = 42–63

What does responsible and ethical deployment of AI tutoring look like in Brazilian public schools — from the perspectives of students, teachers, parents, and administrators?

Literature Context

UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (2021) and its Guidance for Generative AI in Education (2023) established global frameworks for responsible AI deployment in schools, including a recommended minimum age of 13 for student interaction with generative AI. In Brazil, the LGPD requires processing of minors' data in their "best interest," and the 2025 Digital ECA mandates data protection impact assessments with penalties up to BRL 50 million per violation. Yet most AI ethics research in education is theoretical or survey-based, conducted in North American and European contexts. No study has used in-depth qualitative methods to explore how LLM-based conversational tutoring — qualitatively distinct from older hint systems — is experienced, trusted, and governed in Latin American school settings.

Research Questions
  • RQ1: When do students perceive AI tutoring as supporting their autonomy vs. undermining their ability to think independently? What boundaries define responsible AI use from the learner's perspective?
  • RQ2: Under what conditions do teachers view AI tutoring as a complement to their practice rather than a threat — and what safeguards do they require for ethical integration?
  • RQ3: What data privacy and safety concerns do parents express, particularly regarding LGPD and Digital ECA compliance for minors interacting with generative AI?
  • RQ4: What institutional barriers (infrastructure, policy, equity) and enablers do administrators identify for responsible AI deployment in their schools?
  • RQ5: How do stakeholder conceptions of ethical AI use evolve as they gain firsthand experience with the system?
Participants
  • Students: 20–30, stratified by grade, gender, math ability, location
  • Teachers: 8–12 mathematics teachers, varying technology experience
  • Parents/Guardians: 10–15, stratified by SES
  • Administrators: 4–6 principals or coordinators
Data Collection

Semi-structured interviews at 3 time points (pre, mid, post). Student focus groups with think-aloud protocol. Weekly teacher reflection journals. Consent process documentation (questions, concerns, decline rates as data). All in Portuguese, transcribed verbatim.

Analysis Plan

Reflexive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006; 2022). Two independent coders, dialogic consensus. Framework matrices tracking theme evolution across T1, T2, T3. Full CEP approval via IBILCE/UNESP or UFRN. LGPD and Digital ECA compliance.

Integration with Quantitative Studies

Three mixed-methods functions: Explanatory (if Study 1 finds ZPD-adaptive superior, interviews may explain the mechanism). Contextual (teacher/admin perspectives explain implementation fidelity). Discovery (emergent themes generate hypotheses — e.g., "AI helping me think" vs. "AI thinking for me").

Expected Themes
  • The autonomy boundary: Where does "AI helping me think" cross into "AI thinking for me"? Identifying the design features that keep students on the right side.
  • Trust calibration: "How do I know the AI is right?" — and how should the system be designed so students don't blindly trust incorrect outputs?
  • Privacy and data governance: Low student awareness vs. high parental concern. Implications for architectural privacy (protection by design, not by policy).
  • Teacher role in the loop: What level of teacher oversight, control, and transparency is needed for AI tutoring to be ethically deployed?
  • Equity and differential benefit: Does AI tutoring narrow or widen the gap between advantaged and under-resourced students? What safeguards ensure equitable access?
  • Institutional readiness: Infrastructure, policy, and capacity constraints that determine whether responsible deployment is even feasible in Brazilian public schools.

Design principle: Hold everything constant except the variable under investigation. All students use the same app, the same question bank, and the same gamification system. Null results carry the same practical weight as positive results — each study produces actionable findings regardless of which hypotheses are supported.

The Gap Caramelo Fills

No competitor in the Brazilian ENEM space does all four:

  • Micro-habit UX: 1–3 questions/day, 5 minutes. Tiny commitment, massive consistency. Competitors require hours of video.
  • Science-backed learning: Retrieval practice, spaced repetition (FSRS), mastery learning, interleaving — all backed by research with measured effect sizes. Competitors optimize for engagement metrics, not score improvement.
  • Experimentation flywheel: Rigorous RCTs measuring actual ENEM performance. No competitor runs experiments on learning outcomes.
  • Practice-first (not video-first): Testing IS the learning event (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006). Questions first, explanations after.
